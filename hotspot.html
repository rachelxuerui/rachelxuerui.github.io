HotSpot
App designed for User Interface Design with Andy Wang, Teji Vijayakumar, and Theo Mack

LOW FIDELITY MEDIUM FIDELITY HIGH FIDELITY


Overview

HotSpot is an app created for the Semester Project of the course User Interface Design taught by Brian A. Smith.

From the course syllabus, “The Semester Project (S.P.) is a team project to design a digital interface such as a Web-based tool that can enhance the lives of a specific user population in a meaningful way. You get to define the user population any way you wish. Your design should address a need or set of needs that your user population faces that existing tools and solutions do not address.”

Problem

Though apps that share reviews/photos/information about places already exist (ex. Google Maps, Yelp, etc.), they are often impersonal and overwhelming.

Solution

We also would like to consolidate the word of mouth/friend suggestions into one app compared to different places (notes app, text messages, social media, etc.). By consolidating these suggestions, we aim to help users minimize the time they spend looking for a place or spot to go that they also ultimately enjoy.

Target user population

Groups of friends in New York City looking to share recommendations for spots (restaurants, bars, parks, museums, etc) around a geographical area, as well as individuals with friends who wish to quickly find spots to go to.

Initial contextual inquiry

We conducted an initial contextual inquiry via Google Forms and received 26 responses.
  • Most people discover new places through word of mouth, and are taken to new places/experiences by their friends
  • Respondents who live in New York City are often overwhelmed by the many options available to them
  • 84.6% of respondents are either “likely” or “very likely” to recommend a place they like to their friends

Low fidelity prototype

Our initial prototype was a map marked with spots suggested by a user’s friends. To reduce the amount of options on a user’s map, we decided to implement a suggestion algorithm. Upon startup of the app, only places high on the algorithm are displayed with a large, visible geotag. Every other suggestion is minimized to a small dot, combatting the problem of overwhelming choices, without taking away agency from the user, as it displays multiple choices that the user may enjoy.


HotSpot main screen, paper prototype


Additionally, we added a filter button intended to filter the choices via tags. This would allow users to narrow down their displayed options, such as constraining their options to a specific neighborhood or price range.


HotSpot main screen, filter button opened, food filter selected, Absolute Bagels expanded upon, paper prototype


Medium fidelity prototype

Before working on a medium fidelity prototype, we conducted an evaluation with our low fidelity prototype. In this evaluation, we had users interact with our paper prototype without explaining how to use the app, asking them to state aloud how they feel interacting with the prototype while using it. This allowed us to understand the importance of each feature to the user, as well as how easy each feature is to use and how it may be improved.

One of the main issues we uncovered through our low fidelity prototype evaluation was that though users expressed a desire to filter their options, they did not use the provided “filter” button. Thus, our updated design solution was designed to draw users to choose to filter by three modes: “food”, “drink”, or “fun”. These modes, when selected, filter the map and reduce it significantly to reduce user choice, while also shifting the color theme to put it in a specific “mode”.


HotSpot main screen, Figma prototype


We also experimented with more filtering options, as users found searching for spots using certain categories to be a useful potential feature. Categories that users have shown us are important to them include price, cuisine type for food, friends, and location. Thus, we experimented with price filters, cuisine filters, and bar vibe filters.


HotSpot main screen, filters iterated, Figma prototype


We also added static frames exploring a feature for commenting, as users expressed a need for more social engagement. Our hope was that this would allow users to engage socially in the app without feeling overwhelmed by excessive social functionality.


HotSpot review screen and comments screen, Figma prototype


We also added functionality to filter reviews, as one user pointed out that this would be useful (when responding to “How could this page be improved”, said “filter reviews”). Thus, we added functionality to the prototype such that clicking either the “love”, “like”, or “okay” icons would filter the reviews accordingly, and would be signified by being filled in with color. Clicking them again would remove the color and undo the action, revealing all reviews again.


HotSpot review screen with filtering, Figma prototype


High fidelity prototype

Our final design solution centers on three screens - a main screen, a "review" screen, and a "suggest" screen. The main navigation screen is designed to minimize the decisions a user has to make, thus preventing a feeling of overwhelmingness by doing the following:

Location pins
Our app has two different types of location pins for spots on the map. One is a classic geotag pin shape that is slightly larger, and the other is a small dot. Our app has an algorithm that determines which suggested spots users are most likely to like, which determines which spots appear as a larger geotag. The other spots are just spots the users’ friends suggest, but not recommended by the algorithm, marked on the map as dots. This draws the users to select the larger geo tags they are more likely to like, thus reducing decisions they have to make and preventing them from feeling overwhelmed.

HotSpot location pins


Filters
Our app has “food,” “drink,” and “fun” filters, as well as price filters that users can crossfilter to find exactly what they are looking for. Furthermore, these filters reduce what appears on the screen, thus decreasing the potential for users to be overwhelmed by what shows up on their screen.

HotSpot main screen, Figma prototype


Review summaries
For the spot review page, when a user clicks on a spot, a preview pops up that shows them a review summary (“loves,” “likes,” and “okays”), two reviews, and pictures, so that they can quickly see relevant information. This allows them to quickly move between the map and the review without being jumped from page to page, also reducing the risk that they will be overwhelmed. If they want to continue reading the reviews for a spot, they can swipe up and see more reviews, and they can also filter reviews based on “love”/”like”/”okay”, and bookmark a spot.

HotSpot "review" screen, Figma prototype


We changed our method for review filtering as users found it to be unintuitive. After a lot of iteration, we settled on a design with a separate option to filter reviews, and double checked that this was useful and more intuitive through our final contextual inquiry.

HotSpot "review" screen filtering, Figma prototype


Suggesting
Users can enter their review, rate their experience based on whether they “love,” ‘like,” and “okay,” the spot, and add pictures. We designed this app such that users can only suggest spots, rather than review all spots, to further minimize the content that a user might have to sift through.

HotSpot "suggest" screen, Figma prototype


Profile
We also designed a profile screen, where users can see their reviews, their bookmarked spots, and their friends. Here, they can also add friends.
