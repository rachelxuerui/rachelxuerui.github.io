    Apartment in Sutton Place

A project for a friend. The apartment has a dining room window that looks onto the Queensboro Bridge at the level of traffic. While most would find it a nuisance, the friend thinks it's a mesmerizing asset. (80% of being a good architect is just having cool friends.) To intensify the view's effect, the pair of double-hung windows is replaced with a large pane of glass — an urban version of Liz & Ric's “screensaver” window at the ICA. Next to the picture window, a wall separating the dining room and the kitchen is clad with aluminum panels to reflect diffused lights from the bridge at night. Behind the aluminum wall, a new kitchen is made of hand-polished McMaster-Carr parts, strung together by custom 3D-printed fittings, and topped with polished marble. The apartment had eccentric bathrooms — one cladded with mirrors, one with wallpaper, another with tiles. Rather than imposing new designs, we are remaking them into more extreme versions of their previous selves: the mirrors become larger, more extensive, and seamless; the wallpaper more immersive; the tile joints continuous and intentional.
As of , we are nearing the end of a brutal construction process. First the management company had a staffing shuffle halfway through the application. Then the building fired their reviewing engineer. Then we learned that the DOB had mistakenly slapped a violation onto our building for an incident at the adjoining building down the street, which froze all permits. Then my friend got a new job and moved halfway across the world. When we finally got to starting work, we uncovered mold, asbestos, jerry-rigged heating systems, and perfectly good pipes that the building made us replace because the last owner did a poor job closing a wall. This set off a months-long dispute between my friend and the co-op board. Lawyers got involved. By the time that was sorted, so much of the already tight budget had been spent on repairs, remediations, and lawyer fees that we had to cut, delay, or redesign portions of the project, mid-construction. Against all odds the project is looking good, and the owner is still a friend — which is not nothing.
