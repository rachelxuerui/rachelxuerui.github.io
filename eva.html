Eva - purchase decision chatbot
Helping consumers confidently make purchase decisions with an AI powered chatbot
Eva was my primary project for my Product Design Internship with ExpertVoice, an e-commerce platform connecting brands and deals with experts.

I was tasked with the responsibility of conducting user research to identify a problem that our users have, and proposing a solution to that problem through design.

Throughout the project, I had the opportunity to present my progress, findings, and designs to the ExpertVoice executive team, an incredibly exciting opportunity.

How might we combine brand and Expert content into an experience that helps people find and learn about the best products?
PROBLEM
Recent research indicates that most users come to ExpertVoice with an openness to shop for new products.

It is difficult to make decisions between many products when there is a large amount of information that must be sifted through.
"It isn't that shopping on Expertvoice is hard, it is just that there are so many great options, it is hard to decide."
This phenomenon is fairly common, and more well known as the Paradox of Choice.
BRAINSTORMING
During my internship, there was one piece of emerging technology that had been gaining increasing popularity - ChatGPT. This generative AI model could take in text inputs and perform sentiment analysis, text summarization, and draw conclusions.

With this in mind, I played around in ChatGPT, feeding it reviews, product descriptions, and asking it questions about products in different contexts.
QUESTION
Would the Ghost 15 be good for my job as a nurse with plantar fasciitis? Please provide supporting quotes from reviews.

OUTPUT
RESEARCH INSIGHTS
I conducted 30 minute user interview sessions with 5 ExpertVoice users.

INITIAL DESIGNS
I recruited 5 ExpertVoice users for usability testing.
DESIGN APPROACH
  • Provide an introduction to Eva using anthropomorphism to build trust
  • Explain Eva's capabilities and use cases
  • Include details about AI and data usage for transparency
INSIGHTS
  • The Yeti is intimidating and off-putting
  • Too many slides in the introduction
  • Users appreciate the information about data usage
  • Gray button style appears disabled
DESIGN APPROACH
  • Encourage users to shope through product cards
  • Provide supporting evidence for Eva's suggestions via review cards
  • Encourage users to read ExpertVoice lessons for more information
INSIGHTS
  • Grayed out button style for relevant reviews does not seem clickable
  • Relevant reviews are nice to have and build user trust in Eva
  • Method for scrolling is awkward
FINAL DESIGNS
Updated designs using insights from usability testing and the ExpertVoice Product Design team.
Search integration
Recent data collected using Scuba and hotjar revealed that search is the primary action from the main navigation page. Thus, I decided to integrate the primary call to action to access Eva with the search bar, with potential secondary calls to actions where users may feel stuck in their shopping process.
Introducing Eva
Provide transparency about AI, data usage, and inform users of Eva's purpose.
Special thanks to Sage Turk for designing the anthropomorphic (and adorable) representation of Eva.
Suggestion prompts
Quick primary actions to help users understand Eva's purpose and functionality, and encourage them to ask Eva for a recommendation
Product suggestions
Utilize Expert reviews, product information, and other content to provide Experts with valuable product recommendations, weighing more complete reviews more heavily
Supporting Expert content
Provide quotes and links to relevant reviews and additional content to promote trust in product suggestions
Feedback mechanisms
Provide a method of improving the chatbot through user feedback, as well as encourage a feeling of control in users

WHAT I LEARNED
01
User research
This was my first experience with interviewing complete strangers. It was scary, but I learned a lot about professionalism, acting under pressure, and how to successfully conduct open-ended interviews.
02
Stakeholder presentations
I presented my findings and progress to the executive team four times throughout the project. I learned how to put together a comprehensive and effective presentation, speak coherently to a team of people with much more expertise than me, and embrace feedback with open arms.
03
User research
I learned that good designs don't just have to look nice and be useable - they must benefit the company in some way. In our case, buy buy buy! Throughout my project, I learned to consider not only the user, but also encourage them to buy and checkout as much as possible.
