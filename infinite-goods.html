Overview

Infinite Goods is an eco-friendly e-commerce platform supported by the Columbia Build Lab, curating sustainable brands for customers, rating these brands on their sustainability, and educating consumers on sustainability and sustainable fashion in one platform.

As a product designer for Infinite Goods, I am responsible for the design of the website, including new pages and features.


Process


Methodology

As a company with a primary goal of scoring our brands based on their sustainability, we also wish to be transparent with our consumers on how we develop our eco-labels. Thus, I took on the project of designing our Methodology Page.

I initially designed it to be a long page walking users through how each category on the eco-label is ranked, shown below.


Methodology page, initial design, Figma mockup


However, seeing as the eco-label has 13 categories, this would be a very long and monotonous page that users would be subjected to scroll through. Thus, I opted for a design that allows users to explore the categories on the eco-label by directly interacting with the label. Clicking on any category leads to a popup with more information on how that category is scored.

This project is currently on hold as we redesign our eco-label.


Methodology page, final design, GIF of Figma prototype




Who we are

As our initial "Who we are" page only included information about our founder, I chose to redesign this page, showcasing our whole team and our prior relevant experience. I had a few iterations for this design, which I presented to our team for feedback.


Who we are page, initial iterations, Figma mockup


After presenting these three designs to the full team, I received feedback that they liked V1 for it’s larger pictures, and V2 for its clean and minimalistic design. I thus used this feedback to synthesize the larger pictures and clean design for V3, which is the final design.

This design is currently being implemented into the website by Han Choi.




Who we are page, final design, Figma mockup


Eco-label redesign

Customer research conducted showed that users did not find our initial eco-label to be helpful. They expressed a need for more a descriptive design, less confusion about eco-label components, and less unnecessary information. letter grades, Thus, I redesigned our eco-label such that it would address these user concerns and needs.
I designed two versions: one in the previous style of a nutrition label, another in a new style that was more consistent with our style guide.

In the new design, I tried to address our users' needs by including descriptive text rather than percentages, adding a graphic visualizing how sustainable a category was, and reducing space by removing the nutrition label bars and sub-grades for each category.

These designs were presented to users, who largely preferred the new design on the right as it was more consistent with the website's style, less related to negative emotions associated with food, less confusing, and communicated more information in less space.


From left to right: original design, re-design of old label, new design


I also designed a few possibilities for how this new style of sustainability scoring might be implemented into a product page. Our team is currently conducting user research on these designs to determine which would best address user needs.
